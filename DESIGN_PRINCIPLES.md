# Project Chimera Design Principles

## Purpose

This document defines the design philosophy for Project Chimera as a game.

## Core Experience

Project Chimera should feel dangerous, funny, strange, theatrical, systemic, and surprisingly coherent.

The player should feel that the world is bigger than they are, but not arbitrary.

## Emotional Pillars

### Humor

Humor should emerge from character, consequence, systems, contrast, timing, callbacks, and commitment to absurd premises.

The world should commit to the bit. If healing potions become mayonnaise, that joke must affect items, NPC reactions, economy, combat, and consequences.

### Creativity

Players should be rewarded for clever solutions, weird combinations, social manipulation, environmental use, and rule mastery.

### Silliness

Silliness is welcome when it has structure. Random silliness evaporates. Consequential silliness becomes memory.

### Darkness

Darkness gives weight to humor and victory. The world may be brutal, but should not become nihilistic sludge.

### Dialogue

Dialogue should be characterful, reactive, quotable, context-aware, and memorable. NPCs should have opinions, memory, misunderstandings, grudges, biases, jokes, and changing relationships.

### Quests

Quests should never default to empty task lists. A good Chimera quest should include story, agency, consequence, twist, choice, or memorable payoff.

## Consistency And Rule-Breaking

Rules matter. Breaking a rule can be excellent, but only when the break has one or more of the following:

- Discoverable reason
- Narrative purpose
- Systemic cause
- Joke payoff
- Buff or debuff explanation
- Sponsor manipulation
- Faction interference
- Lore revelation
- Player-caused consequence

## Story Tell Value (STV)

Every major feature should be evaluated for Story Tell Value.

A high-STV feature creates moments players want to describe later.

Factors:

- Surprise
- Consequence
- Humor
- Emotional weight
- Emergence
- Shareability
- Replayability
- Agency

## Memorability Index

Features should be scored for:

- Surprise
- Delight
- Emotional weight
- Replayability
- Shareability
- Emergence
- World impact

## Experience Heatmap

Floors, quests, bosses, and sessions should be evaluated across emotional dimensions:

- Wonder
- Humor
- Suspense
- Difficulty
- Mystery
- Relief
- Curiosity
- Frustration
- Triumph

The game should balance emotional cadence, not just combat difficulty.

## Quest Design Requirements

A major quest should answer:

1. What story does it tell?
2. What choices does the player make?
3. What changes because of it?
4. What can go wrong?
5. What can be solved creatively?
6. What might players remember?
7. What rule, faction, character, or system does it teach?

## Dialogue Design Requirements

Dialogue should be checked for:

- Voice uniqueness
- Context awareness
- Repetition
- Exposition dumping
- Humor fit
- Emotional consistency
- Player history
- Faction perspective
- Memory and consequence

## Surprise Types

### Discovery

The player learns something exists.

### Consequence

The player realizes they caused something.

### Revelation

The player learns a truth that changes prior understanding.

### Apparent Rule Break

The player thinks a rule was broken, then later discovers why it was not arbitrary.

## Design Motto

The game should be wild enough to surprise you and consistent enough to earn your trust.
